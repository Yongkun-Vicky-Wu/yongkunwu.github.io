Flow, Unreasonable Tears—The Porcelain Girl’s Monologue

Father used to trade original lily-white porcelain with the Bulgarians. Sharp-edged, sleek porcelain when smashed into pieces. How on earth he managed to do business with those people with his clumsy English did not concern me as much as the porcelain itself. Sharp-edged, yet sleek, porcelain.

He is a savvy-business fund manager who, like any sober man, regarded the porcelain as nothing more than a trade product. That was what I thought until I had this quaint dream back a few years ago. I was, for some reason, alone in a sultry, buggy (all the chirping and squeaking), and bushy forest, surrounded by the Brisbane heat (why it was Brisbane, I had no idea). Out of nowhere, I noticed an apparition lying on the surface covered by dry branches and fallen leaves. It was a pure-white porcelain bowl, intact and uncontaminated, utterly incongruous with the ambiance. Then everything went motionless, the insects stopped cheeping and the breeze no longer rustled the leaves. The next thing I knew, my tears were soaking my pillowcase. I never figured out the dream thence, but an intimate connection between me and the porcelain seemed to have revealed itself.

There had to be an underlying cause, some cause, that triggered all my manic tears over the past few months. Was it the unlikely dreamy peace among the unsettled mess? Or the monotonous heart badly yearning for fluctuant, dramatizing emotions? Only partially. It all boiled down to the trade route of the porcelain, which had been cut off on account of a string of unpredictable events. So the porcelain was packed in transport cases stacked in the warehouse, isolating from the natural lighting that was supposed to shine through shop windows, where they belonged.

Halfway to the ‘destination’ shrouded by thick fog, I seemed to have compromised to what would befall me, whether or not it is a conditional offer. I remembered how the myth of the result accompanied by anxiety and edginess haunted me. I gripped tightly to the outcome, as if all my social values were dictated by the sheer ‘congratulations!’, and wept over trivial things like a flash of unfriendliness or acerb retorts of his. Then it struck, proclaiming that I wasn't suitable for politics, which I instinctively knew. Not attaining full acceptance from all the schools I applied for had involuntarily undermined my conviction of reaching a specific end and propelled me to loosen my grip on the desires, or at least I thought it did. The setback (and possibly the subsequent setbacks) was, in nature, a denial of the unrealistic ego I had built to guard my tattered pride, which was as sharp and fragile as thin porcelain chips.

One should either endure an uncertain, futile and costly high school life or an apparently torturing, conflicting one. I chose the latter, which turned out just fine, despite all its agonizing and absurd nature. Unanimous effort, equally dull. I wouldn't have seen so many peculiar phenomena had I taken the other path. Tears, bliss, loathing, hatred...sentiments that spurt up beyond my expectation had drowned me more often than not. I had pondered turning to a psychologist whose research focused on toxic perfectionism when I was at my tough valleys, defeated by League Tables, and preyed to insecurities. And I had struggled to come to terms with the fact that not a few people support the mass slaughter, as if it were a trivial game that enriched daily chats, citing that it was ideologically and geopolitically righteous, and was heart-broken to discover how our basic morality had sunk into the mire of toxic ideologies and dehumanizing big words. I repeatedly hinted that I did not genuinely care about a war that was not even remotely relevant to my personal interests, for I had been vigilant not to be fooled by ‘kitsch.’ Affecting solicitude was a disgusting form of the often-times elitist posture.

I would judge people based on prejudice and instinct and exaggerate the traits that I found unpleasant. Perhaps I had a twisted perception of what true beautiful humanistic qualities were since I rarely saw any. I should’ve been sentenced to imprisonment if antagonism were a crime. Those people seldom fussed about the subtle ups and downs, assuming they were privy trifles (indeed they were) and were nonchalant about their weaponized and offensive language. They put hard work on the pedestal and excessively took their current success for granted. I admit that I craved for honor, yet I disdain the unreal ‘me’ basked in vanity and anyone who innocently claimed that honor was minor in establishing their identity while pride was emanating from their manners. They cherished leisure, leisure enclosed by guilt. Yet given a choice, they would still opt for a so-called correct and dryly-bustling-around-for-no-reason way of life rather than an adventuring one, despite all their glamorous fantasies. Seize the moment and its most straightforward joy, you might argue. Yes, probably.

The other day, I, for the umpteenth time, cried about some petty things and got confused myself.

“Why the tears?” so I asked.

“It’s self-esteem,” he said.

Which is why he couldn’t be irritated with or mad at me.

“Miserable,” he added.

“Who?”

“Me.”

I laughed, “I love it that you are miserable in this way.”

A non-Beauvoir-style view of love. Absolutely exclusive, soul and flesh. I should’ve been more self-serving, at least not the type of tight-fisted girlfriend who would tentatively observe whoever he was conversing with.

“Hilarious.”

Dear Hell

Matilda was an ordinary schoolgirl, as normal as one could get, well-behaved, friendly, and chimed in occasionally in girl talk; after all, she lacked the grit to play otherwise. For her, the rush for running away peaked whenever her way of getting things sorted out by immersing herself in a make-believe calm state got trespassed. That is, when she was infuriated or saddened or disgusted by what was happening around her. The escape destination could be the States, where she was fascinated by Asian American girls’ natural charm and where the front garden trampoline, the kids’ park they’d go with the host-family kids who comforted her that “No one’s mean” in school, the semi-elliptical clearing where parents parked and picked up elementary school kids still lingered over her. Or it could be New Zealand, where she used to turn somersaults on the lawns. Anyhow, she never expected it to be Australia.

Matilda started vomiting when the plane heading for Brisbane flew over the equator. The cup of instant noodles she brought along to replace the airplane food would go into waste because nausea had preceded hunger unless she was to endure another long flight before it expired. Her last trip to Brisbane five years ago, also accompanied by overwhelming sickness, was a world different; at least she did not need to fumble around for her luggage with a fragile body alone. Which was better, solving endless solid geometry or suppressing the impulse to throw up in the cab (do Australians call it a cab?) that took her to an apartment on Barramul Street, she could not tell. Or to hell. And the hazardous image of her getting raped and murdered on a desert island kept coming back to her until the familiar crossroad and the Sports Park across Woolworths came into view.

The late-coming relief took over her as she stood on the ground floor under the two-story flat. She pushed the luggage onto a slight slope that led to the elevator, pressed the button to the second floor, and realized that she had no keys. It was late into night, and it would be intruding to knock lest a grumpy roommate (if she ever has one) be interrupted. So she dialed the landlord, a retired man living not far away (so she assumed). A minute or two later, the door opened. A girl not much older than herself with fluffy hair dyed in light brown (Matilda hated the hair color), ardent, pinky lips, and swollen sleepy eyes. Korean? Or Chinese? Matilda murmured ‘thanks’ (the Sleepy Eye said she was Chim in turn), briefly introduced herself as the ‘other’ tenant, and slipped into the room.

Out of politeness, Matilda asked whether she’d like any of her salted porridge for breakfast. Amused, Chim asked if she was Chinese. Yes, Matilda said. But somehow, they did not converse in their mother language.

“Haven’t had porridge for years.” She accepted Matilda’s offer.

“Well, only on special occasions. I have a bad stomach.”

“So...for college?” Chim was drinking her share.

“Applying for one, yes. You?”

“Nothing. Wandering around, reading, trying on new clothes and makeup and stuff.”

Matilda did not probe further. The nonchalance Chim displayed seemed to have undermined the toughness of Matilda’s decision, which made her feel stupid. She instinctively mocked at Chim’s ignorance, ignorance about what absurdity she had been through. Had Chim experienced her life, she would hesitate to say she lived to try on clothes. But then Matilda thought, she could wear fancy earrings and put on immature makeup now.

“I will go for a walk now,” Matilda blurted out to Chim once she put the pots and bowls in the dish-washing machine.

Matilda did not bother to take out a map. Hazy memories, like a missing goat coming out of the fog, grew clearer. Once out of the apartment building, she turned right and went up a hill where houses lined alongside the street. The street? Did it use to be a narrower path separated by trees, or did she get the direction wrong? Then a sudden stomach cramp attacked Matilda, reminding her that all she had in the past 20 hours were mouthfuls of insipid porridge. The pain was, without doubt, worsened by the scorching sun in the late morning and the effects of jet lag. Her drained body was yearning for the bed. Why was she here, halfway to the top of a slope? Her unresponsive brain hasn’t comprehended the whole fact that she was no longer in ‘that’ place where purpose was stuffed in every scribble and hurried stride. No one would ever dare invent the legitimacy to spy, eavesdrop, tattle, or secretly photograph. And no violators would be thrown into the abyss of guilt, shame, and horror. Matilda was not obliged to do anything, not even visit the past, and lying in bed all day was as natural as having cereal with milk for breakfast.

When Matilda came back, Chim was reclining on the daybed in the roomy balcony, reading, basking in the sun. Matilda went straight into her room and figured she would stay up till noon and take a nap, to adjust.

Matilda woke up to the clashes of pots and dishes at 5 pm. A sound sleep, no dreams. Dazed and thirsty. She grabbed a cup of water in the kitchen, where Chim was fiddling with carrots, fresh scallions, andouille, and pasta.

“Did I wake you?” Chim cut the carrots into small pieces and put them into the boiling water, in which the pasta was softening.

“No, no. It’s about time.” The cold water Matilda swallowed sent a shudder through her.

“So...how is it here?”

Before Matilda could utter a word, Chim said, more to herself, wryly, “God, can’t believe I asked such a stupid question.”

“Quite the same as I’d expected. I was too weak to look around anyway.”

“It’s the kind of place that seeps into you. You can’t say it’s love or anything.”

“You’ve been here for long?” Matilda regretted the way she asked Chim. It sounded as if she were questioning her knowledge.

Chim didn’t seem to mind, “A while. I move a lot. To try new stuff.”

“There were things I couldn’t stand, so...” Matilda was working her language carefully. She wanted Chim to get a glimpse of things but did not want her to think she was making a big deal out of it.

“Sure there were.” Chim nodded light-heartedly, taking it as a natural fact, then removed the cooked pasta to a colander.

“By the way, I will leave for the South Island in a couple of days.” She added.

“The one in New Zealand?”

She acknowledged it and added oil to the heated pan.

“Are you staying?”

“Guess not. Can’t say for sure. I will keep renting here though. You’ve been there?”

So she had no plans, Matilda thought. “Yeah, when I was thirteen.”

“What do you recall?”

“I bought a plush sheep for fifteen dollars and there was a once-in-a-year sheep parade that day.”

Chim laughed, “Fair enough.”

They were silent for a moment when Chim was adding garlic and bolognese sauce to the pasta.

“If you hear me cry or scream at night, just ignore it and don’t panic. These things happen; sorry if I disturb you.” The tone was less careless.

“What?” At first, Matilda did not grasp it.

“You never know.” Chim turned off the heat and stared right into Matilda’s bewildered eyes.

“Would you like some?” Chim was referring to the noodles.

“I’d love to.”
